# Genre Rules

Use this reference after diagnosis and before rewriting whenever the passage
has a recognizable academic or adjacent-academic genre.

## Abstract

- Preserve contribution, method, data, and result boundaries tightly.
- Remove ceremonial framing and broad "this study provides" language unless it
  carries real information.
- Do not expand claims beyond what the abstract already supports.

## Introduction

- Reduce stock scene-setting language when it delays the real problem.
- Keep the research question, motivation, and scope visible.
- Prefer concrete problem statements over generic significance claims.

## Literature review

- Preserve distinctions among cited authors, methods, and findings.
- Do not collapse differences into smooth generic summary prose.
- Remove repetitive transitions if the citation logic remains clear without
  them.

## Methodology or modelling

- Rewrite conservatively.
- Keep notation, variable definitions, and procedural precision intact.
- Target the surrounding exposition first; standard technical phrasing is often
  appropriate here.

## Results

- Keep reported outcomes, comparisons, and uncertainty statements exact.
- Remove filler around observations, but do not rebalance the evidence.
- Prefer direct statements of what changed, improved, failed, or remained
  stable.

## Discussion or conclusion

- Preserve hedging where the evidence is conditional.
- Remove broad recap sentences that only restate obvious implications.
- Watch for generated-sounding "broader significance" claims that exceed the
  actual findings.

## Proposal, statement, or application materials

- Reduce polished-template rhetoric more aggressively than in standard paper
  sections.
- Replace self-branding phrases with specific aims, interests, methods, or
  experiences.
- Keep the tone formal, but allow a clearer authorial voice than in a journal
  article.
